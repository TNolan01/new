A brief history of SEGA's greatest console

The 1983 Crash

The 1983 crash of the home console market left many companies bankrupt and removed Atari, one of the largest companies, from the market.

Sales were so bad that Atari destroyed unsold games, buring them in the New Mexico desert. The only player left stand was Nintendo, holding a 90%+ share of the market.

Innovation

Largely unaffected by the crash in US, the large electronic companies in Japan continued to develop home consoles. NEC, Nintendo, Microsoft Japan and Sega all developed consoles for the home market.

Back in America, Atari struggled with crippling debt, as sales continued to plummet many believe there was no market for gaming consoles.

SEGA

SEGA Japan were looking to move from the arcade market. SEGA's intial system, branded the Master System, had an 8bit CPU. Although slightly more powerful than the Nintendo it had no real market impact.

As Nintendo's dominance continued SEGA went back to design. In Japan NEC had started to develop a 16bit machine. SEGA followed.

A star is born

In October 1988 SEGA released the Mega Drive to the Japanese market. Well recieved by critics the launch was largely over shadowed by the release of Super Mario Bros 3 on the Nintendo.

Copyright forced the SEGA to launch in America as Genesis. Struggling for a distributor, SEGA decided to use their largely dormant SEGA US company to launch the product.

SEGA heads Stateside

The Genesis hit the US in October 1989 and immediatley met oppostion. Nintendo's dominance ment that software companies were reluctant to license games to SEGA. Distributors loyal to Nintendo were reluctant to stock the Genesis.

SEGA struggled to gain market traction. A new approach would be needed.

Corporate Values

SEGA decided to seek celebrity endorsement, paying sports and entertainment star for naming rights to create their own unique catalogue of games. The project proved successful with many of titles and series still be sold today.

By 1991 SEGA US were still unhappy with sales, they hired Tom Kalinske. Kalinske had massive success as CEO of toy maker Mattel but his 'western' approach to business, pricing and attack advertising put him in conflict with SEGA Japan.

War Begins

Although Both Japanese companies by origin SEGA US saw itself as free from the culture and approach of its parent company in Japan. Shedding Japanese business culture and tradition SEGA US set about directly attacking Nintendo.

Under Kalinske, SEGA US began aggressive TV and print advertising attacking the technically inferior NES. This approach pitted SEGA US against Nintendo AND SEGA HQ in Japan.

Attitude Required!

SEGA US decided they needed their own mascot to challenge Mario, Nintendo's must iconic creation. Enter Sonic.

Sonic had speed, attitude and aggression and he was here to take down Mario. The aggressive marketing and SEGA's newest creation, Sonic, started to find fans in America.

Success

SEGA had captured the youth market. By the start of 1993 SEGA had a 55% share, a massive difference to 1990 when Nintendo controlled a 90% share.

In retaliation Nintendo launched their successor to the NES, their own 16bit machine named the Super Nintendo or SNES for short. The two consoles battled on but trouble was coming.

MEGA CD

SEGA US had reinvigorated a stale industry, forced Nintendo to take notice and altered the endorsement and game franchise market forever.

To future proof SEGA created an add-on CD drive for the Genesis. The CD was seen by the software industry as the next step to over come the data limits of cartridges.

The Next Gen

SEGA got their next generation console, The Saturn, to market in 1995. A CD based console, it was a replacement for the successful Genesis. The Saturn struggled in the US and when Nintendo launched the N64 in 1996 it quickly out sold the SEGA.

Regarded as a commercial disaster the Saturn was SEGA's response to newest kid on the block - Sony.

The Beginning Of The End

Starting life as a joint venture between Sony and Nintendo the Playstation 1 was launched in December 1994 by electronics giant Sony after Nintendo pulled out.

The Sony Playstation was a massive leap over the competion and Sony's arrival into the game console market would mark the begining of the end for SEGA.

Dream On

SEGA discontinued the Saturn in 1998, a commercial disaster it was replace by the Dreamcast. However, neither console could match the success of the Genesis/Mega Drive.

In 2001 SEGA ceased production of the Dreamcast. The financial costs of the failed Saturn and Dreamcast weighed heavily and SEGA withdrew from the console business.
